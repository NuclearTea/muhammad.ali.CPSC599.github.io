Description

The hope with this musical instrument was to channel the experience of your childhood (or even adulthood) in swinging a lightsabre along with gripping it various ways to produce music, allowing the user to not only make sounds using the capacitive touch sensor (which is where I got stuck) but also the movement of the entire hilt to create, and enhance the sounds produced

Ideation

I had a couple of ideas in the duration of this project. Below, I will share some images and quick descriptions of them. I went with sketching as my primary mode of exploration as I am currently taking another class (CPSC 581, Human Computer Interaction 2) where this is a focus and I thought that it would be a way to get some ideas out

    Here are some initial ideas I had. They display some of the different ideas I was toying with, some of which I prototyped, and tried to implement in other ideas

  • Beat Boxing

    A box which had sensors around (photoresistors) and a sonar sensor in the back (which could detect distance) and puncing in different areas of the box would create a different sound. Punching closer to the back (near to the sonar sensor) would change the pitch. An attempt at using our various degrees of freedom along with depth perception to create music. Also, a potential fun workout.

  • Musical Chairs

    Sensors on the floor which would react to being covered by chairs It was an attempt to use our sense of area and coverage to map and modulate sound production

  • Pencil Kalimba

    A pencil with capacitive touch sensors which would map to a kalimba. It would be a bit more versatile as you could also tap the pencil to produce the sound

    • I ended up going with the idea of PVC Pipe lined with Copper wire

  • PVC Capacitive Sensor

    A PVC Pipe lined with Copper wire with an aluminium foil surface to act as a capacitive surface which would map to different sounds

    A light would eminate from the PVC (like the lightsabre) which would be represent the current pitch

    A sound sensor would detect the movement of the PVC (via airflow and friction) allowing for further musical effects
